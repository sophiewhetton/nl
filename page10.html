Op Reis Naar Jou

Met dank aan Willy Sommers en voor het vak 'onderzoek drie' zijn wij op reis geweest naar Jou in Spainje.  Het project is nog in opbouw op een vollwaardig documentaire te worden in boek formaat.  Hieronder zie je alvast de fotos met enkele stukken verhalen in het Engels.

At seven am we found ourselves waiting for the bus to La Guingueta in the station of Barcelona Nord. Breakfast was coffee in a polystyrene cup. Nicolas smoked his final cigarette before the five hour drive began. It had been pouring with rain but he managed to maintain his ‘look’ despite a sleepless night in the hostel of Americans turning on lights and tripping over vodka bottles. He hadn’t brought any walking shoes or rain jacket, I cursed over and over that he would suffer on the mountains in his city clothes. I suppose those practicalities had escaped him. 

Butter for the bread also became an issue. ‘Butter’ I repeated a few times slowly to the dinner lady, ‘cow?’ I tried ‘milk?’, no luck, the working men looked at us confusedly and handed us some olive oil.
Ambient bird sounds accompanied dinner that we assumed came from the cafes speakers but on looking up we discovered an actual bird had made its home right above the mens dining table. Undeterred they continued to enjoy their dinner, the threat of bird poo landing on their food ever present.

We arrived in Jou by foot and rejoiced when we saw it was as picturesque as we had imagined, the sun was shining, the mountains surrounded us in breathtaking splendour, we had drunk our coffee and smoked our cigarettes; all was well. It didn’t even disappoint us that there were no people in Jou, besides one man who fed the thirty or so feral cats roaming the village. I could hear my mother’s voice telling me not to stroke foreign cats because of the fleas and rabies. I told Nicolas this theory and we had a good laugh whilst happily munching our foreign bread and cheese. We laughed too at the unlikely situation of travelling so far to visit a seemingly empty holiday village.
In our optimistic state we begun a hike we’d planned through the mountain. After some hours of happy climbing and laughing in the snow we realised we had lost the main path. The snow began to get higher and beyond our knees but believing we’d find the path again we continued steadily onwards and stupidly, upwards. By four o’clock panic set in. With just a couple of hours left of daylight we had to make our way down a massively steep mountain. Becoming quite aware of the one sandwich we had with us and our wet socks I asked Nik to check the map and compass one more time. I could see he was pissed off with all the map and compass talk, ‘I don’t know how to read this’ he replied, ‘what the hell did they teach you in the Chiro then?’ I snapped back. A couple of hours of sliding on our asses, slipping in snow and clinging to breaking branches we’d begun to envision our own deaths.

We were quite good buddies by the time of Nicolas’s return and he offered to buy us a second round. An offer not to be refused naturally. Nicolas learnt how to say ‘I love you’ in Catalonian and I quickly realised all the locals assumed us to be a couple; it hadn’t occurred to me before but it must have looked very much that way, a man and a woman holidaying together in the mountains in a romantic small cabin. Who would guess the truth of our Willy Sommers motivations?

A woman in a gas station saved us somehow, trusting us to get into her car. It’s a miracle she did as we must have appeared strange at that moment, eyes wide from smoking marijuana, loud and drunk and me limping. I don’t know anymore if I imagined this next part, but she told us a story of her as a new born baby suffering many bouts phenomena and how her mother cured her by walking many kilometres in the mountains with the baby strapped to her back. With visions of magical mountain air and it’s healing properties I fell asleep in our little cabin with the light on.
